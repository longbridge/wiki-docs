# Earnings Power Value

<ArticleMeta id={102191} updatedAt={'2024-09-12 17:31:52'} alias={`[]`} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

<p>Earnings Power Value (EPV) is a financial metric used to assess the intrinsic value of a company by estimating its current value based on its future sustainable earnings. EPV is based on the company's current earnings and assumes that these earnings will remain constant in the future. Unlike traditional price-to-earnings (P/E) or net present value (NPV) methods, EPV focuses more on the company's existing earning power and business stability rather than future growth potential.</p><p>The formula for calculating Earnings Power Value is:<br/>EPV = Adjusted&nbsp;Net&nbsp;Earnings/Capitalization&nbsp;Rate<br/><br/>where adjusted net earnings are the company's current net earnings adjusted to a sustainable long-term level, and the capitalization rate is typically the company's required rate of return or discount rate.</p><p>Key characteristics include:</p><p>Focus on Current Earning Power: EPV emphasizes the company's current level of profitability rather than future growth projections.<br/>Simplified Assumptions: Assumes that the company's current earning power will continue, simplifying predictions of future uncertainties.<br/>Conservative Valuation: By not considering future growth, EPV often provides a relatively conservative valuation.<br/>Broad Applicability: Suitable for evaluating mature and stable companies, especially those with limited growth potential.<br/>Example of Earnings Power Value application:<br/>Suppose a company has adjusted net earnings of $50 million and a capitalization rate of 10%. The company's EPV would be:<br/>EPV = 50&nbsp;million&nbsp;USD/0.10 = 500&nbsp;million&nbsp;USD</p>
