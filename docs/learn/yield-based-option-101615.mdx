# Yield-Based Option

<ArticleMeta id={101615} updatedAt={'2023-09-25 22:58:58'} alias={`[]`} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

A yield-based option allows investors to buy or sell calls and puts on the yield of a security rather than its price.

<AIContent content={`<p><strong>Definition:</strong> Yield options are financial derivatives that allow investors to buy or sell call and put options based on the yield of a security rather than its price. They are typically used for hedging interest rate risk or for speculation.</p><p><strong>Origin:</strong> The concept of yield options originated in the 1980s, as financial markets became more complex and the demand for interest rate risk management increased. The earliest yield options were primarily applied in the bond market.</p><p><strong>Categories and Characteristics:</strong> Yield options are mainly divided into two categories: yield call options and yield put options.<ul><li><strong>Yield Call Options:</strong> Grant the holder the right to buy a security at a specific yield at a specific time. Suitable for scenarios where yields are expected to rise.</li><li><strong>Yield Put Options:</strong> Grant the holder the right to sell a security at a specific yield at a specific time. Suitable for scenarios where yields are expected to fall.</li></ul>The main characteristics of these options include:<ul><li>Trading based on yield rather than price.</li><li>Used to hedge interest rate volatility risk.</li><li>Applicable to bonds and other fixed-income securities.</li></ul></p><p><strong>Specific Cases:</strong><ul><li><strong>Case 1:</strong> An investor holds a large amount of long-term bonds and is concerned that rising interest rates will lead to a decline in bond prices. The investor can purchase yield call options to hedge against the risk of rising interest rates.</li><li><strong>Case 2:</strong> A bank expects interest rates to fall in the future and wants to profit from it.
