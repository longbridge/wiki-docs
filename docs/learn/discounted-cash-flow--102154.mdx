# Discounted Cash Flow 

<ArticleMeta id={102154} updatedAt={'2024-09-12 02:59:41'} alias={`[]`} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

<p>Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) is a valuation method that assesses the intrinsic value of a company by discounting its future cash flows to their present value using a specific discount rate. The DCF method is based on the time value of money principle, which states that future cash flows are worth less than current cash flows and thus need to be discounted. This method is widely used for company valuation, investment decisions, and project evaluation.</p><p>Key characteristics include:</p><p>Time Value of Money: The DCF method is based on the principle that future cash flows are worth less than current cash flows.<br/>Discount Rate: A suitable discount rate (usually the Weighted Average Cost of Capital, WACC) is chosen to discount future cash flows to their present value.<br/>Future Cash Flows: Predicting the future cash flows of a company or project over several years and discounting them to their present value.<br/>Terminal Value: Calculating the terminal value of the company at the end of the analysis period and discounting it to present value.<br/>Calculation steps:</p><p>Forecast Future Cash Flows: Predict the future cash flows for several years based on the company's financial status and market prospects.<br/>Choose Discount Rate: Determine an appropriate discount rate (such as WACC) that reflects risk and capital cost.<br/>Calculate Present Value: Discount the forecasted future cash flows and terminal value to their present value using the discount rate.<br/>Total Present Value: Sum all the discounted cash flows to obtain the total present value, which is the intrinsic value of the company.<br/>Example of Discounted Cash Flow application:<br/>Suppose a company has the following forecasted future cash flows:</p><p>Year 1: $1 million<br/>Year 2: $1.2 million<br/>Year 3: $1.4 million<br/>Assume a discount rate of 10% and calculate the terminal value. Using the DCF method, calculate the present value of future cash flows and sum them to determine the company's intrinsic value.</p>
