# Unamortized Bond Premium

<ArticleMeta id={102147} updatedAt={'2024-09-12 18:01:30'} alias={`[]`} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

<p>Unamortized Bond Premium refers to the portion of the bond premium that has not yet been amortized as of a specific date. When a bond is issued at a price higher than its face value (at a premium), the premium is gradually amortized over the life of the bond, reducing the bond's book value. The amortization can be done using either the straight-line method or the effective interest method.</p><p>Key characteristics include:</p><p>Premium Issuance: The bond is issued at a price higher than its face value, resulting in a premium.<br/>Gradual Amortization: The premium is gradually amortized over the bond's life, reducing its book value.<br/>Financial Statements: Unamortized bond premium is listed as a reduction to long-term liabilities on the balance sheet.<br/>Interest Expense Impact: The amortization of the premium adjusts the interest expense reported on the income statement, effectively reducing the interest expense paid.<br/>Example of Unamortized Bond Premium application:<br/>Suppose a company issues a bond with a face value of $1,000 for $1,050, resulting in a premium of $50. The bond has a term of 5 years, and the premium will be amortized over these 5 years. If the company uses the straight-line method, it will amortize $10 of the premium each year. At the end of the first year, the bond's book value will be reduced by $10, resulting in a book value of $1,040, with an unamortized bond premium of $40.</p>
