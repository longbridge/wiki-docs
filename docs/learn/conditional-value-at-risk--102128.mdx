# Conditional Value At Risk 

<ArticleMeta id={102128} updatedAt={'2024-09-12 03:07:09'} alias={`[]`} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

<p>Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR), also known as Expected Shortfall or Tail Value at Risk (TVaR), is a risk management metric that measures the risk of extreme losses for financial assets or investment portfolios. CVaR goes beyond Value at Risk (VaR) by not only considering the probability of loss corresponding to VaR but also focusing on the average loss when losses exceed the VaR threshold. In other words, CVaR represents the expected loss given that the loss exceeds the VaR level, providing a more comprehensive assessment of tail risk.</p><p>Key characteristics include:</p><p>Risk Measurement: CVaR measures the loss beyond the VaR at a given confidence level.<br/>Extreme Losses: Focuses on tail risk, i.e., the most extreme potential losses.<br/>Comprehensive: Provides a more comprehensive assessment of extreme risk compared to VaR.<br/>Wide Application: Widely used in financial risk management, portfolio optimization, and insurance.</p><p>Example of CVaR application:<br/>Suppose a bank's investment portfolio has a 99% confidence level VaR of $1 billion, meaning there is a 1% chance that losses will exceed $1 billion. CVaR calculates the average loss in those worst-case scenarios. If the CVaR is calculated to be $1.2 billion, this indicates that in the worst 1% of cases, the average loss is $1.2 billion. The bank can use CVaR to develop more effective risk control strategies, ensuring financial stability in extreme market conditions.</p>
