# Disposable Income

<ArticleMeta id={101487} updatedAt={'2023-09-25 22:58:54'} alias={`[]`} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

<p><span style={{backgroundColor:"rgb(255,255,255)",color:"rgb(17,17,17)"}}>Disposable income is the amount of money that an individual or household has to spend or save after federal, state, and local taxes and other mandatory charges are deducted. Economists closely monitor disposable personal income as a key indicator of the strength of the economy. Also known as disposable personal income or </span><a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/netincome.asp"><u>net income</u></a><span style={{backgroundColor:"rgb(255,255,255)",color:"rgb(17,17,17)"}}>, It includes both necessary spending on essentials like food and rent and discretionary spending on leisure and luxury items.</span></p>

Disposable income is also known as disposable personal income or net income.</p><p><strong>Origin:</strong> The concept of disposable income originated in the early 20th century when economists began to focus on the actual consumption capacity of households and individuals. With the improvement of the tax system and the development of economic theories, disposable income gradually became an important indicator of economic health.</p><p><strong>Categories and Characteristics:</strong> Disposable income can be divided into two categories: one is the expenditure on basic necessities such as food, rent, and medical expenses; the other is discretionary spending on leisure and luxury items.
