# Consumer Credit

<ArticleMeta id={101036} updatedAt={'2023-09-25 22:58:54'} alias={`[]`} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

<p>Consumer credit, or consumer debt, is personal debt taken on to purchase goods and services. Although any type of personal loan could be labeled consumer credit, the term is more often used to describe unsecured debt of smaller amounts.</p>

<AIContent content={`<p><strong>Definition:</strong> Consumer credit, or consumer debt, is personal debt incurred to purchase goods and services. While any form of personal loan can be considered consumer credit, the term is more commonly used to describe smaller, unsecured debts.</p><p><strong>Origin:</strong> The concept of consumer credit dates back to the late 19th and early 20th centuries, when the industrial revolution led to a significant increase in the production of consumer goods. As consumer demand for goods and services grew, banks and financial institutions began offering various forms of loans to help consumers enjoy goods and services in advance.</p><p><strong>Categories and Characteristics:</strong> Consumer credit is mainly divided into two categories: unsecured loans and secured loans.<ul><li><strong>Unsecured Loans:</strong> These loans do not require any form of collateral and typically include credit card debt, personal loans, and student loans. They are characterized by fast approval but higher interest rates.</li><li><strong>Secured Loans:</strong> These loans require collateral, such as auto loans and home loans. They are characterized by lower interest rates, but if repayments are not made on time, the collateral may be seized.</li></ul></p><p><strong>Specific Cases:</strong><ul><li><strong>Case One:</strong> John wants to buy a new computer but lacks sufficient funds. He chooses to use a credit card for installment payments, which is a typical example of unsecured consumer credit. This way, John can enjoy the convenience of a new computer in advance while easing his financial burden through installment payments.</li><li><strong>Case Two:</strong> Jane plans to buy a new car but does not have enough cash to pay in full.
