# Relative Vigor Index 

<ArticleMeta id={102158} updatedAt={'2024-09-12 17:41:29'} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

<p>The Relative Vigor Index (RVI) is a technical analysis indicator used to measure the price trend and market momentum of stocks or other financial assets. The core idea behind the RVI is that in an uptrend, the closing price tends to be higher than the opening price, and in a downtrend, the closing price tends to be lower than the opening price. The RVI calculates the average of price changes over a period and compares it to the average of the price range, assessing the market's vigor and trend strength.</p><p>Key characteristics include:</p><p>Calculation Method: The RVI compares the difference between closing and opening prices, averages these differences, and then ratios them to the average of the price range.<br/>Smoothing: To reduce the impact of short-term fluctuations, the RVI is often smoothed (e.g., with a moving average) to produce more stable trend signals.<br/>Trend Indication: An RVI value above 0 indicates an uptrend, while a value below 0 indicates a downtrend.<br/>Cross Signals: Crossovers between the RVI and its signal line can serve as buy or sell signals.<br/>Example of Relative Vigor Index application:<br/>Suppose a stock has an RVI value of 0.6, and the RVI line crosses above its signal line. This indicates that the stock may be in an uptrend, and a technical analyst might interpret this as a buy signal, considering taking a long position. Conversely, if the RVI value is -0.4 and the RVI line crosses below its signal line, this may be a sell signal, prompting the analyst to consider shorting or selling the stock.</p>
