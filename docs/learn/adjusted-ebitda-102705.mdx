# Adjusted EBITDA

<ArticleMeta id={102705} updatedAt={'2024-09-11 23:44:51'} alias={`[]`} />
<div className='border-solid border-b border-t-0 my-4 border-[var(--ifm-color-gray-300)]' />

<p>Adjusted EBITDA (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization) is a measure computed for a company that takes its earnings and adds back interest expenses, taxes, and&nbsp;depreciation&nbsp;charges, plus other adjustments to the metric.</p><p>Standardizing EBITDA by removing anomalies means the resulting adjusted or normalized EBITDA is more accurately and easily comparable to the EBITDA of other companies, and to the EBITDA of a company's industry as a whole.</p>

It builds on traditional EBITDA by excluding unusual or one-time items to provide a more accurate picture of the company's operational performance.</p><p><strong>Origin:</strong> The concept of EBITDA became widely used in the 1980s, primarily for leveraged buyouts (LBOs) and private equity investments. Adjusted EBITDA was developed to address differences in financial statements between companies, offering a more comparable financial metric.</p><p><strong>Categories and Characteristics:</strong> Adjusted EBITDA can be categorized based on different adjustment items, such as excluding one-time legal fees, restructuring costs, or non-recurring income. Its characteristic is to eliminate these unusual items, making financial performance more comparable across companies.</p><p><strong>Specific Cases:</strong> Case 1: A company incurs one-time restructuring costs in a particular year, significantly impacting that year's EBITDA. Case 2: A company receives non-recurring income in a particular year, which does not represent its ongoing profitability. Adjusted EBITDA excludes this income, providing a more accurate assessment of the company's profitability.</p><p><strong>Common Questions:</strong> 1.
